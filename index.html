Section 01

Clinical Psychology,
Neurobiology & Mentoring

Ordered from biological substrate → theoretical framework → developmental context → applied tools. The tiers compound: each layer is built on the one before it.

Tier 1 Biological & Neurological Foundations
01
The Polyvagal Theory
Stephen W. Porges · 2011
W. W. Norton & Company
▾
Why this is first

The single most foundational addition to this list. Polyvagal Theory explains why co-regulation works at a physiological level — neuroception, the vagal brake, the hierarchy of the autonomic nervous system. Your own regulation is not a soft skill; it is a biological instrument. Underpins everything Fonagy, Siegel, and Voss describe without knowing they are describing the same system.

02
Developmental Cognitive Neuroscience: An Introduction
Mark H. Johnson & Michelle de Haan · 2015 (4th ed.)
Wiley-Blackwell
▾
Why this is here

The structural map of the developing brain from birth through adolescence. Foundational empirical neuroscience before clinical application. The "why" behind every clinical intervention with young people.

03
Rutter's Child and Adolescent Psychiatry
Thapar, Pine, Leckman et al. (Eds.) · 2015 (6th ed.)
John Wiley & Sons
▾
Why this is here

The clinical reference standard. Dense and encyclopaedic — not a cover-to-cover read — but the authoritative empirical map of the full diagnostic and developmental landscape you work in. The benchmark against which all applied texts should be checked.

04
Inventing Ourselves: The Secret Life of the Teenage Brain
Sarah-Jayne Blakemore · 2018
PublicAffairs
▾
Why this is here

Rutter translated for the practitioner. Blakemore bridges rigorous neuroscience into the lived experience of adolescence — risk-taking, identity, social sensitivity, peer influence. The most readable entry point to the empirical neuroscience of the cohort you mentor.

Tier 2 Mentalization & Psychodynamic Theory
05
Affect Regulation, Mentalization, and the Development of the Self
Fonagy, Gergely, Jurist & Target · 2002
Other Press
▾
Why this is first in tier

The canonical theoretical text. Fonagy at full theoretical depth — the developmental origins of mentalization, why affect regulation and reflective functioning are inseparable, and how early relational failures produce the clinical presentations you will have managed in NHS acute settings. Read before any of the applied Fonagy material.

06
Inside Lives: Psychoanalysis and the Growth of the Personality
Margot Waddell · 2002 (Rev. ed.)
Karnac Books
▾
Why this is here

The most accessible entry into object relations and Kleinian developmental theory. Where Fonagy gives you the empirical architecture, Waddell gives you the emotional phenomenology — what it feels like to be a developing person. Essential for understanding the adolescents you work with from the inside out.

07
Multiple Voices: Narrative in Systemic Family Psychotherapy
Renos K. Papadopoulos (Ed.) · 2001
Routledge
▾
Why this is here

Bridges psychodynamic theory into the systemic and narrative frame. Particularly relevant for inner city youth work where family systems are complex, fragmented, or adversarial. Sits between the Fonagy theoretical core and the applied AMBIT framework.

08
Adaptive Mentalization-Based Integrative Treatment (AMBIT)
Bevington, Fuggle, Cracknell & Fonagy · 2017
Oxford University Press
▾
Why this is here

Theory translated into a team-based, community-facing system of care. The most directly applicable clinical framework for working with hard-to-reach young people outside formal clinical settings — i.e., exactly what park-bench mentoring requires. Read Fonagy (2002) first.

09
Mentalization-Based Treatment for Adolescents: A Practical Guide
Rossouw & Fonagy · 2021
American Psychiatric Association Publishing
▾
Why this is last in tier

The most applied end of the Fonagy school. A practical clinical manual for MBT-A. Read last in this tier — it assumes the theory and system above.

Tier 3 Youth Development, Adversity & Context
10
Man's Search for Meaning
Viktor E. Frankl · 1959
Beacon Press
▾
Why this is first in tier

Foundational before the others in this tier because it establishes the primary motivational question: why engage with anything at all? When a disengaged inner city adolescent has no reason to engage with life, logotherapy is not philosophy — it is the only framework that directly addresses the clinical obstacle. Meaning as the core human driver.

11
Mindset: The New Psychology of Success
Carol S. Dweck · 2006
Random House
▾
Why this is here

The most glaring omission from the original list. Growth vs fixed mindset is the operational framework of effective youth mentoring — particularly with young people who have been institutionally designated as failures. The research is robust; the practical application is immediate.

12
Scattered Minds: The Origins and Healing of Attention Deficit Disorder
Gabor Maté · 2019
Random House
▾
Why this is here

Maté's central argument — that ADHD is substantially a developmental trauma response rather than a fixed neurodevelopmental trait — directly reframes how you approach the disengaged, dysregulated young people in your caseload. The NHS acute population and inner city London mentoring cohort have enormous ADHD-trauma overlap.

13
The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog
Bruce D. Perry & Maia Szalavitz · 2017 (Rev. ed.)
Basic Books
▾
Why this is here

Perry's Neurosequential Model of Therapeutics in narrative form. More developmentally focused than van der Kolk, more accessible than the clinical papers. Shows what developmental trauma looks like as a lived experience — essential for understanding your mentoring cohort's histories.

14
How Children Succeed: Grit, Curiosity, and the Hidden Power of Character
Paul Tough · 2012
Houghton Mifflin Harcourt
▾
Why this is here

Fills a structural gap nothing else on the list fills. Drawing on economics, neuroscience, and education research, Tough maps why disadvantaged young people succeed or fail. Less clinical than the Fonagy school, more structural. Essential for understanding why inner city mentoring is categorically different from suburban mentoring.

15
iGen: Why Today's Super-Connected Kids Are Growing Up Less Rebellious, More Tolerant, Less Happy
Jean M. Twenge · 2017
Atria Books
▾
Specific to your profile

Your tech background means you will read Twenge's data differently to most practitioners — you understand what she's describing from the architecture side as well as the clinical side. The smartphone generation's anxiety, identity fragmentation, and attention patterns are the actual digital context your mentees are living inside. No other text on this list addresses this directly.

Tier 4 Conversational Tooling & Clinical Bridge
16
Narrative Means to Therapeutic Ends
Michael White & David Epston · 1990
W. W. Norton & Company
▾
Why this is here

The foundational text of narrative therapy. Externalisation of problems, re-authoring conversations, and the therapeutic use of story sit at the heart of effective youth work and connect directly to the systemic-narrative layer in Papadopoulos above.

17
Motivational Interviewing with Adolescents and Young Adults
Naar & Suarez · 2021 (2nd ed.)
Guilford Press
▾
Why this over Miller & Rollnick's original

This adaptation is built specifically for developmental resistance in youth — the ambivalence, the identity investment in non-change, the anti-authority stance. Preferred over the canonical original text for your context because you are mentoring teenagers, not adults in addiction services.

18
Motivational Interviewing in Schools
Rollnick, Kaplan & Rutschman · 2016
Guilford Publications
▾
Why this is here

The most specific application of MI to the environments your mentees actually inhabit. Pairs with Naar & Suarez to give you the full MI toolkit for adolescent work in non-clinical settings.

Section 02

Persuasion, Negotiation
& Charisma

Ordered architecturally — theoretical neuroscience and psychology first, tactical frameworks second, applied behaviours third, power dynamics last. The theory makes the tactics legible; the tactics make the theory usable.

Tier 1 The Architecture of Human Decision-Making
19
Thinking, Fast and Slow
Daniel Kahneman · 2011
Farrar, Straus and Giroux
▾
Why this is first in section

The canonical foundation. System 1 vs System 2 is the theoretical architecture on which every tactical persuasion framework in this section is built. Ariely, Cialdini, Voss, and Klaff are all operating inside the framework Kahneman describes. Read this first and the rest of the section becomes a practical field manual for a single unified theory.

20
Predictably Irrational: The Hidden Forces That Shape Our Decisions
Dan Ariely · 2008
HarperCollins
▾
Why this is here

Where Kahneman is the architecture, Ariely is the field manual. Shows exactly how irrationality operates in pricing, social norms, expectations, and daily decisions — with experiments immediately generalisable to sales, negotiation, and influence. More tactically applied than Kahneman.

21
The Righteous Mind: Why Good People Are Divided by Politics and Religion
Jonathan Haidt · 2012
Pantheon Books
▾
The single biggest gap in the original list

Cialdini tells you how to influence; Kahneman tells you why people are irrational; Haidt tells you why people hold the values they hold. Moral foundations theory is the most powerful persuasion framework that was missing. Essential for cross-cultural, cross-class influence — which inner city London work and commercial negotiations both require constantly.

22
Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion
Robert B. Cialdini · 1984
Harper Business
▾
Why this is here

The applied translation of the Kahneman framework into six universal principles: reciprocity, commitment, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity. The most empirically grounded tactical persuasion text in existence. Read after Kahneman and Ariely — the principles land with greater force once you understand the cognitive architecture they exploit.

23
Pre-Suasion: A Revolutionary Way to Influence and Persuade
Robert B. Cialdini · 2016
Simon & Schuster
▾
Why this follows Influence

Influence maps the six principles. Pre-Suasion covers the moment before the ask — how environmental and contextual priming determines receptivity before a word is spoken. The natural sequel; read immediately after Influence.

Tier 2 High-Stakes Negotiation · Tactical Empathy
24
Getting to Yes: Negotiating Agreement Without Giving In
Roger Fisher & William Ury · 1981
Penguin Books
▾
Why this is first

The academic gold standard and canonical starting point. Principled negotiation — separating the person from the problem, focusing on underlying interests rather than stated positions — is the foundational theory. Read before Getting Past No and Never Split the Difference, both of which are responses to situations this book assumes away.

25
Getting Past No: Negotiating in Difficult Situations
William Ury · 1991
Bantam Books
▾
Why this is here

The field manual for when the other side refuses, stalls, or attacks — the tactical counterpart to the theoretical framework in Getting to Yes. More practically useful in adversarial or emotionally charged negotiations. Bridges directly into the silver tongue skillset.

26
Never Split the Difference
Chris Voss & Tahl Raz · 2016
HarperBusiness
▾
Why this is last in tier

Clinical psychology tools weaponised for negotiation — mirroring, labelling, dynamic silence, calibrated questions. The most tactically dense text in this tier. Read last; it assumes and extends everything Fisher, Ury, Kahneman, and Cialdini establish.

Tier 3 Charisma, Frame Control & Micro-Behaviour
27
How to Win Friends and Influence People
Dale Carnegie · 1936
Simon & Schuster
▾
Why this is first in tier

The list had strong theoretical architecture and was missing the micro-behavioural layer entirely. Carnegie operates at the level of specific conversational moves — genuine interest, active listening, remembering names, letting people feel heard — that Cialdini and Voss assume you already possess. Read before Cabane and Klaff; the higher-level frameworks land better when the foundations are explicit.

28
The Charisma Myth
Olivia Fox Cabane · 2012
Portfolio / Penguin
▾
Why this is here

Charisma decomposed into three learnable components — Presence, Power, and Warmth — and rebuilt as a practical behavioural system. Removes the mythology from magnetism. The natural bridge from Carnegie's micro-behaviours into Klaff's higher-order frame control.

29
Pitch Anything
Oren Klaff · 2011
McGraw-Hill
▾
Why this is last in tier

Frame control at the neurological level. Most pitches fail because they address the neocortex; all information is filtered first by the amygdala. Klaff's STRONG framework teaches you to win the neurological filter before logic enters. Read last in this tier — requires the micro-behavioural and charisma foundations to execute properly.

Tier 4 Power, Dark Arts & Practitioner Regulation
30
The Art of Being Right (Eristic Dialectic)
Arthur Schopenhauer · c.1831 (posthumous)
Various editions
▾
Why this is first in tier

Fundamentally about epistemology and argumentation — the mechanics of how people win disputes regardless of truth. Thirty-eight stratagems for argument. Essential for frame control: both for deploying these tactics and, critically, for recognising when they are being used on you. The dark arts companion on logic and rhetoric.

31
The 48 Laws of Power
Robert Greene · 1998
Viking Press
▾
Why this is here

The translation layer that makes the primary historical sources redundant for a practitioner. Greene synthesises thousands of years of power strategy into modern, applicable laws. Ruthless, amoral, and essential for reading the subtext of office politics, commercial negotiations, and personal branding. Read after Schopenhauer — the laws land with greater force once you understand the dialectical mechanics underneath them.

32
The Obstacle Is the Way
Ryan Holiday · 2014
Portfolio / Penguin
▾
Why this is last on the entire list

The regulation framework for practitioners operating in high-stakes environments. Stoic philosophy stripped of academic weight and rebuilt as a field manual for adversity. The discipline of non-reaction, the reframing of obstacles as material, the acceptance of what cannot be controlled. Everything Porges describes neurologically, the Stoics described philosophically. Read when the rest of the list is complete.
